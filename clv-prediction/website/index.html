01 — Business Problem

Not all customers are worth the same follow-up.

Businesses need to know which customers are worth investing retention and marketing budget in. This project predicts each customer's future revenue over the next 3 months, based on their prior purchase behavior — before that value has fully shown up in the history books.

Instead of a flat, one-size-fits-all marketing spend, the output is a per-customer forecast that can drive segmentation, budget allocation, and early churn intervention.

◆

Identify high-value customers early

Before they've fully proven their worth historically.

◆

Prioritize retention spend

Direct budget where it has the most measurable impact.

◆

Flag likely churners

Surface at-risk customers before they disappear silently.

◆

Segment, don't broadcast

Move from one-size-fits-all to data-driven targeting.

03 — Calibration / Holdout Strategy

Predicting forward, never leaking the future.

Rather than a naive historical CLV sum, the target is genuinely predictive: features come only from the past, the label comes only from the future.

Calibration

Dec 2010 – Aug 2011
9 months · source of all RFM / behavioral features

Holdout

Sep 2011 – Nov 2011
3 months · source of the prediction target

Excluded

Dec 1–9, 2011
Partial month, dropped
Cold-start out of scope — customers appearing only in the holdout period (no calibration history) are excluded from training and evaluation, mirroring how the model would genuinely be used in production.

05 — Feature Engineering

Nine behavioral features, all computed pre-holdout.

Every feature is derived exclusively from the calibration window — nothing from the future leaks in.

Frequency

frequency

Count of past purchases in the calibration window. The single strongest predictor of return.

Monetary

monetary

Total historical spend. Dominant driver of predicted spend amount among returners.

Volume

total_quantity

Total units purchased — a volume signal independent of price.

Breadth

unique_products

Distinct SKUs purchased — a proxy for basket diversity and engagement.

Recency

recency_days

Days since the last purchase as of the snapshot date.

Tenure

tenure_days

Days since the customer's very first purchase — relationship age.

Value

avg_order_value

Mean spend per order — sticky among customers who return.

Flag

is_one_time_buyer

Binary flag for single-purchase customers — a distinct churn-risk cohort.

Geo

is_uk

Binary flag for UK residency, the dominant and best-sampled market.

06 — Model Architecture

The target is zero-inflated. The model architecture should be too.

08 — Feature Importance (SHAP)

What actually drives the prediction.

  • 01

    Frequency dominates Stage 1

    How often someone bought matters far more than recency or total spend for predicting whether they'll return at all.

  • 02

    Monetary & AOV dominate Stage 2

    Past total spend and average order value dominate the spend-amount prediction — spend patterns are sticky among returners.

  • 03

    Geography is a minor signal

    is_uk contributes, but far less than behavioral features — behavior beats geography.

09 — Key Visual Insights

What the exploratory analysis revealed.

Revenue is heavily right-skewed — a log-normal shape that informs the log1p target transform.
Classic Pareto concentration — the top 20% of customers generate 74.6% of revenue.
Purchase frequency — the median customer ordered only twice all year; 25%+ are one-time buyers.
Monthly revenue trend with the calibration/holdout split overlaid, confirming it lands on a strong, non-seasonally-quiet window.
Geographic concentration — the UK dominates at 82.8% of total revenue.
10 — Business Recommendations

Turning predictions into decisions.

01

Segment into 4 tiers

VIP / Upsell / Win-back / Low-priority, based on predicted return probability × predicted spend.

02

Build frequency, not discounts

Reorder reminders and subscription nudges beat one-off discount promos — frequency drives return, not recency or spend.

03

Reallocate budget to the top 20%

They generate ~75% of revenue — flat per-customer spend is measurably suboptimal.

04

Treat 43.9% non-return as a KPI

An urgent, quantified churn metric worth executive attention independent of any model.

05

Fix checkout ID tracking

Closing the ~25% missing-CustomerID gap would materially expand what any future model can see.
